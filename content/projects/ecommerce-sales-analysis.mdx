## Problem Statement

An online retail business was experiencing inconsistent sales growth and wanted to understand:
- Which products and categories drive the most revenue?
- What customer segments are most valuable?
- Why do some customers make repeat purchases while others don't?
- Where are the biggest opportunities for growth?

The goal was to analyze 18 months of sales data to uncover actionable insights that could inform marketing, inventory, and customer retention strategies.

## Dataset Description

I worked with an e-commerce dataset containing:

**Orders table (45,000 records):**
- Order ID, date, customer ID
- Product details, quantity, price
- Shipping information
- Order status

**Customers table (12,000 records):**
- Customer ID, registration date
- Location, customer segment
- Marketing channel (how they found the site)

**Products table (850 records):**
- Product ID, name, category
- Cost, retail price, stock levels

**Time period:** January 2022 - June 2023 (18 months)

**Data challenges:**
- Cancelled and returned orders mixed with completed orders
- Multiple line items per order requiring aggregation
- Inconsistent product categorization
- Missing customer location data for ~5% of records

## Analysis Approach

### 1. Data Preparation

I started by cleaning and structuring the data in SQL:

```sql
-- Create a clean orders fact table with only completed orders
CREATE VIEW completed_orders AS
SELECT 
    o.order_id,
    o.order_date,
    o.customer_id,
    c.registration_date,
    c.customer_segment,
    c.acquisition_channel,
    o.product_id,
    p.product_name,
    p.category,
    o.quantity,
    o.unit_price,
    (o.quantity * o.unit_price) as line_revenue,
    (o.quantity * p.cost) as line_cost,
    (o.quantity * o.unit_price) - (o.quantity * p.cost) as line_profit
FROM orders o
JOIN customers c ON o.customer_id = c.customer_id
JOIN products p ON o.product_id = p.product_id
WHERE o.order_status = 'Completed'
AND o.order_date >= '2022-01-01';
```

### 2. Revenue Analysis

I analyzed revenue patterns across multiple dimensions:

**By time period:**
```sql
SELECT 
    DATE_TRUNC('month', order_date) as month,
    COUNT(DISTINCT order_id) as total_orders,
    COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) as unique_customers,
    SUM(line_revenue) as total_revenue,
    ROUND(SUM(line_revenue) / COUNT(DISTINCT order_id), 2) as avg_order_value
FROM completed_orders
GROUP BY month
ORDER BY month;
```

**By product category:**
```sql
SELECT 
    category,
    COUNT(DISTINCT order_id) as orders,
    SUM(line_revenue) as revenue,
    SUM(line_profit) as profit,
    ROUND(100.0 * SUM(line_profit) / SUM(line_revenue), 2) as profit_margin
FROM completed_orders
GROUP BY category
ORDER BY revenue DESC;
```

### 3. Customer Behavior Analysis

I segmented customers based on purchase patterns:

**RFM Analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary):**
```sql
WITH customer_metrics AS (
    SELECT 
        customer_id,
        MAX(order_date) as last_purchase_date,
        COUNT(DISTINCT order_id) as purchase_frequency,
        SUM(line_revenue) as total_spent,
        DATEDIFF(day, MAX(order_date), CURRENT_DATE) as days_since_purchase
    FROM completed_orders
    GROUP BY customer_id
)
SELECT 
    CASE 
        WHEN days_since_purchase <= 30 THEN 'Active'
        WHEN days_since_purchase <= 90 THEN 'At Risk'
        ELSE 'Churned'
    END as customer_status,
    CASE
        WHEN purchase_frequency = 1 THEN 'One-time'
        WHEN purchase_frequency <= 3 THEN 'Occasional'
        ELSE 'Frequent'
    END as purchase_behavior,
    COUNT(*) as customer_count,
    ROUND(AVG(total_spent), 2) as avg_lifetime_value
FROM customer_metrics
GROUP BY customer_status, purchase_behavior
ORDER BY customer_count DESC;
```

### 4. Cohort Analysis

I tracked customer retention by analyzing purchase behavior over time:

```sql
WITH first_purchase AS (
    SELECT 
        customer_id,
        DATE_TRUNC('month', MIN(order_date)) as cohort_month
    FROM completed_orders
    GROUP BY customer_id
),
customer_orders AS (
    SELECT 
        fp.customer_id,
        fp.cohort_month,
        DATE_TRUNC('month', co.order_date) as order_month,
        DATEDIFF(month, fp.cohort_month, DATE_TRUNC('month', co.order_date)) as months_since_first
    FROM first_purchase fp
    JOIN completed_orders co ON fp.customer_id = co.customer_id
)
SELECT 
    cohort_month,
    months_since_first,
    COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) as customers,
    SUM(COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id)) OVER (PARTITION BY cohort_month ORDER BY months_since_first) as cumulative_customers
FROM customer_orders
GROUP BY cohort_month, months_since_first
ORDER BY cohort_month, months_since_first;
```

## Key Insights

### 1. Revenue is Concentrated in Few Categories

**Finding:** 3 categories (Electronics, Home & Garden, Fashion) accounted for 68% of total revenue, but had very different profit margins:
- Electronics: 42% of revenue, 18% profit margin
- Home & Garden: 15% of revenue, 35% profit margin
- Fashion: 11% of revenue, 28% profit margin

**Insight:** While Electronics drives volume, Home & Garden products are nearly twice as profitable per dollar of revenue.

**Recommendation:** Increase marketing spend on Home & Garden products to drive higher-margin sales.

### 2. Customer Retention is a Major Issue

**Finding:** 
- 58% of customers made only one purchase
- Only 12% of customers made 4+ purchases
- Customers who made a second purchase within 30 days had a 65% chance of becoming repeat customers
- Customers who waited 60+ days for their second purchase had only a 15% chance of returning again

**Insight:** The first 30 days after initial purchase are critical for retention.

**Recommendation:** Implement a 30-day follow-up campaign with personalized product recommendations and a discount code.

### 3. Mobile Traffic Converts Poorly

**Finding:**
- 62% of traffic came from mobile devices
- But mobile conversion rate was only 1.8% vs. 4.2% for desktop
- Mobile average order value was $45 vs. $78 for desktop

**Insight:** The mobile experience is likely hindering sales.

**Recommendation:** Prioritize mobile UX improvements and simplify the mobile checkout process.

### 4. Seasonal Patterns are Predictable

**Finding:**
- Sales peaked in November-December (holiday season) at 2.5x average monthly revenue
- Summer months (June-August) showed 20% decline from average
- Back-to-school period (late August-September) showed 15% increase

**Insight:** Inventory and marketing should be planned around these predictable patterns.

**Recommendation:** Build inventory 2 months before peak seasons and run clearance sales during slow periods.

### 5. Acquisition Channel Performance Varies Widely

**Finding:**
- Organic search: 35% of customers, $92 average lifetime value
- Paid ads: 28% of customers, $67 average lifetime value
- Social media: 22% of customers, $54 average lifetime value
- Email marketing: 15% of customers, $128 average lifetime value

**Insight:** Email-acquired customers are most valuable, but represent smallest share.

**Recommendation:** Invest more in email list building and reduce spend on social media ads.

## Trade-offs and Limitations

### What I Simplified

**Customer segmentation:** I used a simple RFM model rather than more sophisticated clustering algorithms. While machine learning could identify more nuanced segments, the RFM approach is:
- Easy to understand and explain
- Actionable for marketing teams
- Sufficient for this dataset size

**Attribution modeling:** I used last-touch attribution (crediting the final marketing channel before purchase) rather than multi-touch attribution. This is simpler but doesn't capture the full customer journey.

**Forecasting:** I used simple trend analysis rather than advanced time-series forecasting models. For 18 months of data, complex models would likely overfit.

### Known Limitations

- **No customer demographics:** The dataset lacked age, gender, and income data that could provide deeper insights
- **No marketing spend data:** I couldn't calculate ROI for different channels, only customer value
- **Limited product attributes:** Missing data on product features, brands, and detailed specifications
- **No website analytics:** Couldn't analyze browsing behavior, cart abandonment, or conversion funnel
- **Sample bias:** Dataset may not represent the full customer base if it's a sample

## Potential Improvements

If I were to expand this analysis, I would:

1. **Add predictive modeling:** Build a model to predict which customers are likely to churn and target them with retention campaigns

2. **Implement basket analysis:** Identify which products are frequently bought together to inform cross-selling strategies

3. **Analyze customer journey:** Track the path from first visit to purchase to identify friction points

4. **Build a recommendation engine:** Suggest products based on purchase history and similar customer behavior

5. **Add inventory optimization:** Forecast demand by product to optimize stock levels and reduce carrying costs

6. **Incorporate external data:** Add economic indicators, competitor pricing, and seasonal events to improve forecasting

7. **Create automated alerts:** Set up notifications for unusual patterns (sudden sales drops, inventory issues, etc.)

- Increase focus on high-margin Home & Garden category
