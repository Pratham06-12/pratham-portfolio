I'm a final-year Electronics Engineering student specializing in VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration)—basically, chip design. I spent three years learning about semiconductors, circuit design, and digital systems. And now? I'm pursuing a career in data analytics instead.

This isn't a story about hating engineering or making a dramatic career pivot. It's about discovering what kind of work actually energizes me, and being honest about where I want to go.

## How I Got Here

During my third year, I did a Systems Engineering internship at Pantech Prolabs. The role involved analyzing system performance data, identifying bottlenecks, and presenting findings to the team. I expected it to be okay—something to put on my resume.

Instead, I loved it.

I loved digging into data to find patterns. I loved the moment when a visualization suddenly made a complex problem clear. I loved that my analysis directly helped the team make decisions.

Meanwhile, my VLSI coursework felt increasingly disconnected from what I wanted to do. I could design circuits and understand semiconductor physics, but I didn't feel the same spark.

That's when I started asking myself: what if I'm in the wrong field?

## The Honest Realization

Here's what I realized: I like solving problems, but I'm more interested in *business and operational problems* than *technical engineering problems*.

VLSI is fascinating—don't get me wrong. But it requires a deep, specialized focus on hardware that I don't have. The people who excel in chip design are genuinely passionate about transistors and fabrication processes. I respect that, but it's not me.

Data analytics, on the other hand, sits at the intersection of technology and business. You use technical skills (SQL, Python, Power BI) to solve real-world problems (Why are sales declining? Which customers are at risk? How can we optimize this process?).

That's the work I want to do.

## What I'm Learning

Since making this decision about six months ago, I've been teaching myself data analytics while finishing my degree. Here's what I'm focusing on:

### SQL
This has been my primary focus. I've learned:
- Writing complex queries with joins, subqueries, and aggregations
- Query optimization techniques (indexes, execution plans)
- Window functions for time-series analysis
- Data cleaning and transformation

I practice by working with real datasets—customer data, sales transactions, system logs. The more I write SQL, the more I realize how powerful it is for answering business questions.

### Power BI
I've built several dashboards for my projects:
- Credit risk analysis dashboard
- E-commerce sales performance dashboard
- Systems performance monitoring dashboard

I'm learning that good dashboard design is harder than it looks. It's not just about making charts—it's about telling a clear story with data.

### Python (Basic)
I'm honest about this: I'm a beginner with Python. I can write scripts for data cleaning and basic analysis using pandas, but I'm not building machine learning models or writing complex algorithms.

I'm focusing on practical Python for data work—reading files, cleaning data, basic visualizations with matplotlib. I'll go deeper as I need to.

### Excel
People underestimate Excel, but it's incredibly powerful for analysis. I use it for:
- Quick exploratory analysis
- Pivot tables and charts
- Data validation and cleaning
- Prototyping before building dashboards

## The Projects I've Built

To prove to myself (and potential employers) that I can do this work, I've completed three data analysis projects:

**Credit Risk Analysis:** Analyzed loan application data to identify risk factors and build a risk scoring model. Used SQL for data extraction and Power BI for visualization.

**E-commerce Sales Analysis:** Analyzed sales patterns, customer behavior, and product performance for an online retail dataset. Identified key drivers of revenue and customer churn.

**Systems Performance Analysis:** Built on my internship experience to analyze system logs, identify performance bottlenecks, and create monitoring dashboards.

These aren't perfect, and they're not enterprise-scale projects. But they're real analysis work that demonstrates I can extract insights from data and communicate them effectively.

## What I'm Not Doing

I want to be clear about what I'm *not* claiming:

- I'm not a data scientist building ML models
- I'm not a data engineer building pipelines
- I'm not an expert—I'm a transitioning analyst

I see a lot of portfolios that oversell experience, and I don't want to do that. I'm early in my analytics journey, and I'm okay with that. I'm focused on building solid fundamentals and real skills.

## The Challenges

This transition hasn't been easy. Here are the real challenges:

**Explaining the switch:** "Why aren't you pursuing VLSI?" is a question I get a lot. Some people get it, others think I'm wasting my degree.

**Imposter syndrome:** I'm competing with people who have statistics or computer science degrees. Sometimes I wonder if I'm qualified.

**Learning while finishing my degree:** Balancing final-year coursework with self-learning is exhausting. There are days when I question if I'm doing the right thing.

**Job market uncertainty:** I don't know how employers will view my background. Will they see my engineering degree as irrelevant? Or will they value the analytical thinking and problem-solving skills?

## Why I Think This Will Work

Despite the challenges, I'm optimistic. Here's why:

**Transferable skills:** Engineering taught me how to break down complex problems, think systematically, and learn technical concepts quickly. These skills apply directly to data analytics.

**Genuine interest:** I'm not doing this because it's trendy or pays well. I'm doing it because I genuinely enjoy the work. That motivation matters.

**Practical experience:** Through my internship and projects, I've done real analysis work. I'm not just learning theory—I'm building things and solving problems.

**Willingness to start small:** I'm not expecting a senior analyst role. I'm ready to start as a junior analyst or intern and learn from experienced people.

## What's Next

I'm currently:
- Finishing my final semester of engineering
- Building more projects to strengthen my portfolio
- Applying for junior data analyst roles
- Learning more advanced SQL and Power BI techniques
- Starting to explore basic statistics and A/B testing

My goal is to land a junior analyst role where I can learn from experienced analysts, work with real business data, and continue developing my skills.

## Advice for Others Considering a Transition

If you're thinking about switching fields, here's what I'd say:

**Be honest with yourself:** Don't switch just because you're struggling in your current field. Switch because you're genuinely drawn to something else.

**Build real projects:** Theory is important, but projects prove you can do the work. Build things, even if they're imperfect.

**Don't oversell:** Be honest about your skill level. "I'm learning" is better than pretending to be an expert.

**Accept the uncertainty:** Career transitions are uncomfortable. You won't have all the answers, and that's okay.

**Focus on fundamentals:** Don't chase every new tool or technique. Get really good at the core skills (SQL, data visualization, analytical thinking).

## Final Thoughts

I don't know exactly where this path will lead. Maybe I'll end up in business analytics, maybe product analytics, maybe something I haven't discovered yet.

What I do know is that I'm working on problems I care about, learning skills I enjoy, and moving toward work that energizes me. That feels like the right direction.
